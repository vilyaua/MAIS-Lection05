# Comparison of Naive RAG, Sentence-Window Retrieval, and Parent-Child Retrieval

## Introduction / Overview
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) enhances large language models (LLMs) by augmenting them with relevant external knowledge. How the knowledge is chunked and retrieved plays a critical role in the system's accuracy, contextual understanding, and ability to reduce hallucinated answers. We compare three retrieval approaches commonly seen in RAG systems: Naive RAG, Sentence-Window Retrieval, and Parent-Child Retrieval.

## Section 1: Naive RAG
- **Description:** Naive RAG uses simple chunking of documents into fixed-size or overlapping text chunks without much semantic awareness.
- **Chunking:** Text is split into equal-size pieces or with small overlaps, often cutting sentences arbitrarily.
- **Retrieval:** Vector similarity search retrieves the nearest chunks.
- **Advantages:** Simple and easy to implement; cost-effective.
- **Limitations:** Can cut important semantic units, causing loss of meaning and context; splits information across chunks, confusing retrieval; limited context given to the LLM; higher risk of hallucination due to fragmented data.

## Section 2: Sentence-Window Retrieval
- **Description:** Improves on naive chunking by splitting text into sentences and embedding them with a surrounding window of neighboring sentences.
- **Chunking:** Sentences are parsed and their embeddings are augmented with a few preceding and following sentences as context (e.g., one sentence before and two after).
- **Retrieval:** Matches based on sentence embeddings but includes surrounding context to improve semantic cohesion.
- **Advantages:** Maintains semantic units (sentences) intact; adds local context to resolve references like pronouns; better precision, especially in domains needing detail (legal, medical).
- **Limitations:** Slightly more complex; added computational cost for embedding windows rather than isolated sentences; context window size needs tuning to balance precision and semantic dilution.

## Section 3: Parent-Child Retrieval
- **Description:** A hierarchical retrieval method that uses fine-grained "child" chunks for query matching and larger "parent" chunks to provide enriched context.
- **Chunking:** Data is split into small, precise chunks (child) for retrieval; each child is linked to a larger chunk (parent) such as a paragraph or full document.
- **Retrieval:** First retrieves child chunks that precisely match the query, then fetches their parent chunks to provide the LLM with broader context.
- **Advantages:** Balances precision of small chunk retrieval with comprehensive context from larger parent sections; reduces information overload and loss of narrative; particularly useful in complex documents or customer support knowledge bases.
- **Limitations:** More complex pipeline and data management; potential redundancy in retrieval; indexing requires both granular and parent-level embeddings.

## Comparison / Analysis
| Feature                   | Naive RAG                          | Sentence-Window Retrieval               | Parent-Child Retrieval                      |
|---------------------------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------|
| Chunk Unit                | Fixed-size or overlapping chunks | Sentences + surrounding sentences    | Child chunks (small) + Parent chunks (large) |
| Context Preservation      | Low                              | Moderate to High                       | High                                       |
| Precision                 | Low to moderate                  | High                                  | High                                       |
| Implementation Complexity | Low                              | Medium                                | High                                       |
| Suitable Use Cases        | Simple, low-cost setups          | Domains needing precision and detail | Complex knowledge bases needing context   |
| Risk of Hallucination     | Higher                          | Lower                                 | Lowest                                     |

## Conclusion
Naive RAG provides a simple entry point for retrieval-augmented generation but suffers from loss of semantic context and precision. Sentence-Window Retrieval improves retrieval quality by embedding sentences with surrounding context, better resolving references and preserving unit meaning. Parent-Child Retrieval further enhances RAG by combining fine-grained retrieval with contextual enrichment from parent chunks, striking a balance between precision and comprehensive context for complex domains. The choice depends on domain complexity, required precision, and engineering complexity tolerances.
